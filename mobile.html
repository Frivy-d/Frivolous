ARTISTSTATEMENT

My inspiration for the project Frivolous is to retain my innocent fascination with the genre of Electronic Music and engage a naive spirit that communicates through the integration of multi-contextual and multi-historical influences. Ideas of sound re-contextualization (sampling) are used to create unorthodox narratives in unified sonic spatial environments. Although the music can often be danceable, the intention is to provoke mystery and musical questions in the listener as I seek to illuminate the playfulness of the human condition.

HISTORY

My name is Daniel Gardner. My main project is called Frivolous. I participated in (and emerged from) the Canadian underground Electronic Music scene under a very heavy influence from German Minimal House Music (Frankfurt circa late-2000's) and the French and Québécois Mikrohouse movements. Under the name Frivolous I have performed and published original Electronic music which resulted in a substantial body of work published by independent labels around the world. My influences do not adhere to conventional or conservative "genre-fication"; I unapologetically borrow influences from cultures and styles throughout history and weave them into this work.

In addition to the Frivolous work I've produced music on a commission-basis including themes for podcasts and Radio-stations including Deutschland-Radio, TwenFM and worked on a freelance basis at the CBC (Canadian Broadcasting Company) in live-sound. Performing this work live at selected special locations; Frivolous (Live) has been featured at such renowned Festivals as Montreux Jazz Festival, MUTEK, Transmediale/CTM and Amsterdam Dance Event. I currently have a studio in Berlin where I continue to record original music, collaborate with other artists and make remixes while also doing live sound mixing and recording for independent films and at the 'Konzertreihe am Café Tasso' in Berlin-Friedrichshain.

Sound Engineering: my excursion into Sound-Engineering has mostly been unguided and unconventional. Perhaps this is the reason for the unique sound of Frivolous over the years (see sections: New Releases & Discography). My musical journey started with Classical music studies at a young age. I was enrolled at Royal Conservatory of Music where I begrudgingly finished my Grade-8: including Music-Theory and History. Only long after my career was started did I do an MA in 2020 in Creative Music Production at Catalyst Institute in Berlin. This offered me the opportunity to do something productive during the Pandemic and the chance to return to Europe after almost 10 years of living back in Canada.

I came to this program at Catalyst with many metaphorical demons in my closet - these were topics which I was growing more and more uncomfortable with in the evolving landscape of music production, especially as it played-out between 2008-2018. Thankfully the Masters Degree program taught me how to conduct an Academic- and Practice-based research approach to dealing with these concerns which did certainly help me exorcise those demons. I could finally make an academic assertion for my growing discomfort regarding the music of this time. Mainly that the tools being used to achieve LOUDNESS in recorded music was also killing the producers ability to create "reality-based space". I then conducted a material study (more artistic than scientific) to determine if the music of this time (which was becoming known to many technicians as "the loudness war period") was not only evolving in an aesthetic way, but doing so in direct conversation with the "reality" of acoustic spatial design. Playing with these ideas of reality or, at least, referencing the human-being's experience in sound design had up to this point been ubiquitous in sound-engineering and recordings made up until around 2008 in almost every genre including Electronic Music. After which time it seemed engineers were placing less emphasis (consciously or unconsciously) on "reality-based" spatial acoustic modeling techniques in favour of fragmented or "hyper-reality" spatial dimensions. This led to the question: was the competition for loudness impeding music's ability to convey the human-experience or, perhaps in parallel, does a new sonic "hyper-reality" allow listeners a new prospectus: to escape (rather than be reminded of) the reality of resentful human decline. I do not wish to focus on the darkness of this prospect and although I do try to remain unbiased and use my production tools in a way that makes listeners simply enjoy music - I will say that I certainly have a bias towards one of the two options. Let's make this fun! Can you tell which one? (reality or hyper-reality based sound design?) I would let my listeners determine this for themselves.

LIVEPERFORMANCE

Performance Ideology (& Bookings): Frivolous is not an act which is concerned by definitions of commercial visibility. Although some live appearances have happened at notable venues around the world; my focus remains to refine my performances to special and un-usual events. My music pivots between dance-music and something designed for the listener to discover in their own space. Considering that modern 'Club Culture', as it is today, is hard-pressed to provide such a space for sonic discovery: Frivolous (Live) will appear in carefully considered locations only. These include regional venues for dedicated fans who do not have the chance to travel to urban areas, concept events such as hidden stages at Festivals and Community-Projects where sonic and performance explorations remain the primary focus. If you have such an event planned and would like to invite Frivolous (Live) please reach out to Ulrike Schmeisser uli@uliversum-booking.de

PROJECTS

Contraptions — Adopting cutting-edge production and engineering techniques; live performances under the name Frivolous have been defined by my love of building and hacking weird machines. Examples of hand-made instruments used in recordings and live performances include: Robotic xylophones [Bild 1: setting PureData in play to control complex mechanical systems], Bicycle-powered spring-reverb unit with modulating reverb-coils [Bild 2], "The Knife" contact-microphone knife-instrument [Bild 3], "Double-cable Tub Bass", a "broken-ruler music box" and "The Telephone" singing & monitoring-system [Bild 4] simply called 'the Telephone' which is still in use today.
